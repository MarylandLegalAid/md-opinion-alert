About MD Opinion Alert

What this is

Every weekday morning, this app checks mdcourts.gov for newly published opinions — reported and unreported — from the Supreme Court of Maryland and the Appellate Court of Maryland. It searches the full text of each new opinion for the keywords you're watching and collects the results in two places:

  • Your dashboard shows every opinion that has ever matched your keywords, newest first, with filters by keyword, court, opinion type, and date. Matches are shown in context, so you can tell at a glance whether an opinion is worth opening.
  • Email digests deliver just the new matches — opinions the app picked up since your last digest. Daily digests go out weekday mornings; weekly digests go out Mondays. Empty digests are never sent.

How to use it

  1. Add the terms you care about on the Keywords page — your own keywords, shared lists, or both. New keywords are matched against the whole back catalog immediately, so your dashboard fills in right away.
  2. Pick how often you want email on the Preferences page: daily, weekly, or dashboard-only.
  3. That's it. Check your dashboard whenever you like; the email will nudge you when something new matches.

Where this came from

This app was the brainchild of Zafar Shah, our Advocacy Director for the Human Right to Housing. Zafar wanted a reliable way to know, without manually checking court websites, when Maryland's appellate courts said something that touched his work. So he did something a little unusual: he sat down with an AI coding assistant and "vibe coded" a working prototype — no formal spec, no project plan, just a clear idea of what he needed and a willingness to tinker until the prototype did it.

He shared that prototype with John Jeffcott, our IT Director, who volunteered to turn it into what you're using now: a proper internal web app with single sign-on, shared keyword lists, scheduled ingestion, and email digests.

Here's the part we actually want you to take away: you don't need to build a prototype. Zafar's prototype was great, but the thing that made this project happen wasn't the code — it was that he could describe exactly what he needed. "Watch these two courts, match new opinions against my keywords, and email me when something hits." A clear, well-specified idea like that is all it takes to get started.

So if there's a tool you wish existed — something that would save you time, catch things you currently catch by hand, or just make a tedious part of your week less tedious — bring it to IT. Worst case, you'll have an interesting conversation. Best case, you'll be reading the "where this came from" page of your own idea.
